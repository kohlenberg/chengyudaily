[break 0.5s] 口 (kǒu) - mouth [break 0.5s] 蜜 (mì) - honey [break 0.5s] 腹 (fù) - belly [break 0.5s] 剑 (jiàn) - sword [break 0.5s] The full idiom is 口蜜腹剑, which literally means 'mouth honey, belly sword.' Figuratively, it describes someone who speaks sweetly but has malicious intentions. [break 1s] Here’s the story behind it: [break 1.5s] The idiom originates from ancient Chinese literature, where a character would flatter others with sweet words while secretly plotting against them. This highlights the contrast between outward appearances and hidden agendas, a lesson about trust and deception. [break 1.5s] Example 1: 他对我很客气，但我知道他是口蜜腹剑。 [break 1s] He is very polite to me, but I know he is sweet on the outside but has hidden motives. [break 1s] Example 2: 在商业谈判中，要小心口蜜腹剑的人。 [break 1s] In business negotiations, be careful of those who are sweet-talking but have ulterior motives. [break 1s] Example 3: 她的赞美只是口蜜腹剑。 [break 1s] Her praise is just sweet words hiding a sword. [break 1s] So remember, 口蜜腹剑 means sweet words but hidden daggers; deceitful charm.
