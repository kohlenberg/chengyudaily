[break 1s] '闻' (wén) means 'to hear' [break 0.5s] '鸡' (jī) means 'chicken' [break 0.5s] '起' (qǐ) means 'to rise' [break 0.5s] '舞' (wǔ) means 'to dance' [break 1s] So, the full idiom is 闻鸡起舞, which literally translates to 'rise and dance at the sound of a chicken,' and figuratively suggests waking up early to practice or seize opportunities. [break 1s] Here’s the story behind it: [break 1.5s] This idiom originates from the famous story of a man named Wang Xiang during the Eastern Han Dynasty. He was so dedicated to his studies that he would rise early at the crow of a rooster to practice his swordsmanship. His commitment and hard work eventually led him to great success. [break 1.5s] Example 1: 他每天早上闻鸡起舞，努力练习。 [break 1s] He wakes up early every morning to practice hard. [break 1s] Example 2: 只有闻鸡起舞，才能在竞争中获胜。 [break 1s] Only by rising early and seizing opportunities can one win in competition. [break 1s] Example 3: 她的成功是因为她总是闻鸡起舞。 [break 1s] Her success is because she always rises early to take action.
