Welcome to Chengyu Bites — your quick summary on Chinese 成语. This idiom emphasizes the value of firsthand experience. [break 1s] The phrase is: 百闻不如一见, bǎi wén bù rú yī jiàn. [break 1s] 百 (bǎi) means 'hundred' [break 0.5s] 闻 (wén) means 'to hear' [break 0.5s] 不 (bù) means 'not' [break 0.5s] 如 (rú) means 'as' [break 0.5s] 一 (yī) means 'one' [break 0.5s] 见 (jiàn) means 'to see'. [break 1s] So, 百闻不如一见 literally means 'A hundred hears are not as good as one see.' Figuratively, it suggests that personal experience is invaluable. [break 1s] Here’s the story behind it: [break 1.5s] This idiom comes from ancient Chinese literature, where it was often noted that stories could be embellished or misinterpreted. A direct experience, however, provides clarity and understanding. Thus, the phrase encourages individuals to seek out real-life encounters instead of relying solely on hearsay. [break 1.5s] Example 1: 你听说那个地方很美，但百闻不如一见。 [break 1s] You've heard that place is beautiful, but seeing it in person is better. [break 1s] Example 2: 书上的描述很精彩，但我还是想去看看，毕竟百闻不如一见。 [break 1s] The description in the book is amazing, but I still want to see it; after all, seeing is believing. [break 1s] Example 3: 朋友告诉我这个活动很好玩，但我觉得百闻不如一见。 [break 1s] My friend told me this event is fun, but I think experiencing it is the best way to know. [break 1s] So remember, 百闻不如一见 means 'Hearing a hundred times is not as good as seeing once.' Thanks for listening to Chengyu Bites! See you next time for another idiom. [break 1s]
